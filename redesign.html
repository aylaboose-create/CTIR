Coastal Tide
Impact Research

Cal Poly students are mapping how the Central Coast is changing — one tide at a time.

Scroll to read the story

For some, sea level rise might be a distant thing. But in the face of climate change, its effects are already reaching into coastal communities. Sea level rise has led to increased flooding, urban blockages, and sediment buildup in estuaries and bays. We've seen it firsthand — at Toro Creek Bridge, Morro Bay State Park, and other locations in San Luis Obispo. These effects will become increasingly drastic and more common as the climate continues to shift.

It's key that we begin tackling how to design our infrastructure for what's coming, and record what tides look like now so we have a baseline for understanding what will change. That's exactly what CTIR is doing.

The Problem

Tide Gauges in California Are 100 Miles Apart. That's Not Enough.

NOAA specializes in collecting environmental data including tide levels. But gauges are sparsely located — one every 100 miles or so. At a price point of $25,000 each, before installation, the gaps between them are enormous.

The Gaps

Between Point Conception and Morro Bay, Almost Nothing.

That stretch of coastline — bays, estuaries, creek mouths — sits in a data blind spot. Communities in this region are making planning decisions without the local tide data they need to make them correctly.

CTIR's Response

Low-Cost Gauges, Deployed by Students, at Key Locations.

CTIR builds and deploys gauges that cost a fraction of NOAA instruments. From Los Osos Creek to the Cal Poly Pier, the team is filling in the gaps with real, local data.

"This project offers an ability to get across a message to people about how their shorelines might change in the future." — Dr. Serena Lee, Physical Oceanographer
Origin Story

It Started with One Student and an Idea.

When masters student Alex Dunn first joined the project, he fully committed his time to figuring out what made tide gauges tick. No one really knows how he figured out all the nitty gritty details, but he did. With the help of students like Jonathan Mass and Kaden Caliendo, CTIR now had a way to measure tides in San Luis Obispo.

That's the work that Civil Engineering and Water Resources professor Stephan Talke has been trying to do for the majority of his career. After years of research on the history of tide measurements — including post-doctoral work on intertidal mudflats on the border of the Netherlands and Germany — Talke realized how tide measurements were one of the best ways to indicate sea level rise. So he started in San Luis Obispo, recruiting students from his classes to create CTIR.

"We're trying to create a baseline measurement of the coast at many more locations than currently exists." — Prof. Stephan Talke

Photo Essay

A Day Out In the Field

Students deploy a new remote radar gauge at the Cal Poly Pier.

01 / 12

CTIR engages in weekly field work collecting data or deploying new equipment to monitor tidal changes. On May 14th, the task at hand was deploying a homemade Radar Gauge at the Cal Poly Pier.

02 / 12

Eugene Petrov, a fourth year computer engineering major, and Collin Roppe, a second year mechanical engineering major, drive down the lengthy path to the end of the pier. Eugene points out long metal pipes that lined the pier — used to transport oil starting in the early 1900s. It wasn't until 2001, when Unocal donated the pier to Cal Poly's College of Science and Mathematics, that it was used for coastal marine work.

03 / 12

Pier technician Jason Felton greets the team as they roll onto the metal grates. The pier is mostly used by professors and other research projects — technicians like Jason help support students and professors working on site.

04 / 12

CTIR's homemade radar gauge shoots radar signals from the gauge to the water and tracks how much time it takes for the signal to return, telling us how high the water is at different times of day. Eugene's modification allows CTIR to collect data remotely on a laptop — no more opening the gauge to retrieve a tiny SD card.

05 / 12

Eugene and Collin fiddle with the equipment, making sure everything is properly connected, then check whether the sensor is responding on Eugene's laptop. The next task is the more nerve-wracking part of installation: dangling the gauge and its wooden mount over the side of the pier.

06 / 12

Their workspace is a bit cramped. A wooden board beneath their feet ensures nothing gets dropped between the grates 20 feet into the water. Professor Talke brought one of his water dynamics classes to watch — the duo now has an audience to perform for.

07 / 12

Pier manager Tom Moylan chats with Professor Talke's class, reciting the history of the pier. Talke jokes that only one CTIR student has ever fallen into the water at a different deployment location. Hopefully not today.

08 / 12

"Anyone know any sailor knots?" Eugene asks, laughing somewhat nervously as the two begin to fasten the mount to the pier. A rope keeps the mount secured as they place U-shaped bolts around the pier railing. Once the bolts are placed, the hard part is over.

09 / 12

After fastening the bolts to the railing, the fresh gauge sits proudly above the water.

10 / 12

Professor Talke and Collin admire the gauge. The hope is to return in a couple of weeks and evaluate how the homemade gauge fares against the previously installed gauge that lacks remote data collection.

11 / 12

Collin and Eugene thread an extension cord to power the gauge from an outlet on the pier. This eliminates the solar panel battery reliability issue — on cloudy or rainy days, the old gauge would often fail to charge consistently. A big step in data quality.

12 / 12

Eugene pulls up the program that tracks the data from the sensor — and sure enough, the gauge has started taking readings and plotting them into a graph. The team can now access data from anywhere on Cal Poly's WiFi network.

Introduction

Why we're measuring tides

NOAA gauges cost $25,000+ each and are spaced ~100 miles apart, leaving coastal communities without local data on sea level rise. CTIR builds low-cost gauges to fill those gaps — providing the resolution needed for real coastal planning.

Make modular, replicable, low-cost gauges
Increase data resolution along the Central Coast
Measure long-term sea level rise trends
Understand tide patterns within an enclosed bay

Ultrasonic gauge

Emits sound waves downward and times their return from the water surface. Sensitive to temperature and humidity — corrections are an active area of improvement.

TypeIn-house build
LocationsCayucos, San Simeon, Avila
LimitationTemp/humidity drift

Radar gauge

Sends radio pulses to the water surface and measures return time. CTIR's in-house radar at Cal Poly Pier supports remote data collection over WiFi.

TypeCommercial + in-house
LocationsMorro Bay, Cal Poly Pier
AdvantageRemote data access

Pressure gauge (HOBO)

A submerged sensor measures how much water is pressing down on it — more water = higher tide. Compact and easy to deploy in estuaries and bays.

TypeHOBO (commercial)
LocationsLos Osos, Chorro Creek
AdvantageCompact, submerged

How it works

Distance = speed × time

1
Signal emittedThe gauge sends a sound or radio wave downward toward the water surface.
2
Wave reflectsThe signal bounces off the water surface and returns to the sensor.
3
Time measuredThe gauge records exactly how long the round trip took.
4
Distance calculatedDistance = Speed × Time ÷ 2. That distance tells us the water level.

Key formula

Distance = Speed × Time / 2

Looking Ahead

Sea Level Rise Isn't Going Away.

This isn't something that's going to lessen, or go back to the way it was a decade ago. Our coastlines are actively changing, so we must change too. Collecting information about tides may seem mundane, but it's what will help us fight beach loss, save our homes, and continue living in the places we love and want to protect.
