Introduction

Why we're measuring tides

NOAA gauges cost $25,000+ each and are spaced ~100 miles apart, leaving coastal communities without local data on sea level rise. CTIR builds low-cost gauges to fill those gaps — providing the resolution needed for real coastal planning.

Make modular, replicable, low-cost gauges
Increase data resolution along the Central Coast
Measure long-term sea level rise trends
Understand tide patterns within an enclosed bay

Ultrasonic gauge

Emits sound waves downward and times their return from the water surface. Sensitive to temperature and humidity — corrections are an active area of improvement.

TypeIn-house build
LocationsCayucos, San Simeon, Avila
LimitationTemp/humidity drift

Radar gauge

Sends radio pulses to the water surface and measures return time. CTIR's in-house radar at Cal Poly Pier supports remote data collection over WiFi.

TypeCommercial + in-house
LocationsMorro Bay, Cal Poly Pier
AdvantageRemote data access

Pressure gauge (HOBO)

A submerged sensor measures how much water is pressing down on it — more water = higher tide. Compact and easy to deploy in estuaries and bays.

TypeHOBO (commercial)
LocationsLos Osos, Chorro Creek
AdvantageCompact, submerged

How it works

Distance = speed × time

1
Signal emittedThe gauge sends a sound or radio wave downward toward the water surface.
2
Wave reflectsThe signal bounces off the water surface and returns to the sensor.
3
Time measuredThe gauge records exactly how long the round trip took.
4
Distance calculatedDistance = Speed × Time ÷ 2. That distance tells us the water level.

Key formula

Distance = Speed × Time / 2
